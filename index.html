Context A Tuesday with ShareLedger on MyBillBook

MyBillBook is a platform where Indian MSMEs manage invoices and GST-compliant records. At the core of every business relationship on the platform is a party ledger — a running record of every transaction, payment, and balance owed between two parties.

A Tuesday for Ramesh & Aakash.

Ramesh runs a retail shop in Old Delhi. Aakash is his distributor in Northeast Delhi. Aakash creates an invoice, sends it on WhatsApp. Ramesh types it into his own ledger — slightly different totals because he missed a line item. A week later, payment is due. Aakash calls. They compare screenshots. Forty minutes pass. Nothing is resolved.

With ShareLedger: Aakash creates the invoice. Ramesh sees it immediately — same numbers, no typing. He taps "Convert to Purchase." It's in his books. It's done.

Here's the thing — when Aakash creates an invoice, Ramesh still has to enter it manually on his end. Same transaction, two people recording it separately, at different times. By the time payment is due, the numbers are almost never the same. Now One of them will spend the next forty minutes on a call trying to reconcile them. .

03 Insights from research

What we acted on and How

Four pain points that defined the brief
Buyer · Mismatch Anxiety

My numbers and my supplier's numbers are never the same

Buyers reconcile balances manually every payment cycle. A missed line item or wrong date is all it takes to spark a dispute that neither party can resolve quickly.

Buyer · Double Entry Tax

I'm re-entering data my supplier already entered

Every purchase bill a buyer creates is a duplication of the supplier's sales invoice. Most buyers weren't tracking at all — no digital purchase records whatsoever.

Supplier · Trust Erosion

Buyers won't believe me until I send a screenshot

WhatsApp screenshots had become the de facto source of truth — fragile, informal, and impossible to audit. No formal record existed outside each party's own ledger.

Supplier · Invisible Acknowledgement

I don't know if my buyer even saw the invoice

Suppliers create invoices and send them — but have no signal that the buyer acknowledged them. Payment delays often started not with disputes, but with the buyer simply not having the invoice.

Insights we acted on
A Match by mobile number — Phone numbers are how they know each other. Auto-pairing on login with zero setup from either party.
B Read-only is default — suppliers need to trust that the ledger is shared with everyone by default, but what if I want to hide it from a few?
C One-tap "Convert to Purchase" - removes manual friction — buyers shouldn't re-enter what the supplier already entered. Item name mismatches are a mapping problem, not a blocker. Solve the re-entry problem first.
D Discovery must be in-context — a standalone section would be ignored. Entry points needed to live inside the Parties screen where the relationship already existed — and trigger at the moment the user felt the pain.
04A HMW — Auto-pairing & Shared Access
HMW #1

How might we let suppliers share their ledger without feeling like they're giving up control?

Constraint: Sharing must be easy to disable per party or globally with a master switch. Must not require the supplier to do anything special to enable pairing.

01Silent auto-pairing on login+

When a user logs in, the system checks whether any saved party mobile numbers match an existing MyBillBook account. If they do, a "Shared Books" section surfaces automatically — no invite, no setup. The user discovers a shared ledger already exists.

02MBB badge on Parties screen+

Paired accounts display an MBB icon badge. The supplier sees their writable ledger as usual, plus a visual signal that this ledger is now shared. No data changes, no new screens — just a new layer of visibility.

Edge cases
Same phone number, two party names (multi-branch buyer)+

Scenario: A supplier saved the same buyer's number under two different names — two branches.

Resolution: Each record gets its own ledger card. Supplier controls sharing for each card. On the buyer's side, both cards show under the supplier's name, so it's clear they're separate accounts.

Scenario:If shared ledgers appeared for salesperson logins, company financials would be exposed to a limited-access user.

Resolution: Shared Ledger section is hidden entirely for salesperson logins. For buyers who saved a salesperson's number, the MBB icon still appears on the party card so they know a connection exists — but the shared ledger shows "Ledger hidden" rather than surfacing financial data to a non-admin.

04B HMW — Voucher Conversion
HMW #2

How might we eliminate double-entry for buyers without requiring them to set up anything?

Constraint: One-tap conversion; item names may differ between buyer and supplier books — this can't be a blocker.

The flow
01Trigger+

Every sales invoice in the shared view has a single CTA: "Convert to Purchase." It appears only on invoices — not on payment records, where it would be semantically wrong.

02Pre-fill Voucher bills+

Tapping pre-fills a purchase bill with matching items, tax, and date from the supplier's invoice. Where item names match, they auto-map. Mismatches prompt a simple mapping step — not a blocker, not a modal wall.

03Audit link+

Once converted, the source invoice shows a "Converted" badge visible to both parties. The audit link between source and mirror voucher is preserved permanently — either party can trace back.

Edge cases
Invoice moved to wrong party after conversion+

Scenario: Supplier realises they issued an invoice to the wrong party and corrects it. The original buyer has already converted it to a purchase bill. Both parties now have an inconsistent record pointing.

Resolution: Buyer's converted purchase bill remains in their books under the original party — their record is valid. Clicking into the linked source invoice shows an explicit error: "Source invoice has been reassigned.".

04C HMW — Permission & Privacy
HMW #3

How might we give suppliers granular control over what they share without making it feel like a settings maze?

Constraint: Two levels — per-party and global. Salesperson login must never surface the company's shared ledger, even if a buyer saved the salesperson's number.

Control hierarchy
01Per-party+

Toggle in party settings disables sharing for any specific buyer. Buyer sees "Ledger hidden" — not a blank screen. They know the relationship exists even if the data is gated, and can contact the supplier directly from that state.

02Global+

Master switch in global party settings disables sharing for all buyers at once. Useful for businesses that temporarily want to stop all sharing — without having to toggle each party individually.

03Role-based+

Salesperson login type: Shared Ledger section hidden entirely. Even if a buyer saved the salesperson's number, the supplier's financial data is never exposed to a limited-access user.

04D HMW — Phone Number & Data Integrity
HMW #4

How might we handle phone number changes and party deletion without silently breaking ledger links?

Constraint: Existing converted purchase bills must remain accessible even if the source relationship changes. History cannot disappear.

Edge cases — where the real design work was

This product deals with money and trust. The edge cases aren't edge cases — they're the moments that determine whether users trust the system at all. We designed explicit resolutions for four scenarios that would otherwise become support tickets within the first week of launch.

Phone number change — link must not break silently+

Scenario: A supplier updates their buyer's stored mobile number. Previously this would silently sever the ledger link — both parties continue believing the ledger is shared, but it's actually disconnected. Disputes emerge weeks later with no clear cause.

Resolution: When a number changes, new links are created against the updated number and old links are archived (not deleted) to preserve voucher history. Existing converted purchase bills remain accessible. The supplier sees a banner notification confirming the update. Links broken before this fix surface as "link unavailable" rather than showing wrong data.

Supplier deletes the party — buyer still needs history+

Scenario: A supplier settles all dues and deletes a buyer from their party list. The buyer has existing transactions, converted purchase bills, and potential tax obligations tied to those records — they can't just disappear.

Resolution: When a party is deleted, the buyer's shared ledger view shows "Ledger hidden" rather than vanishing. If the supplier re-adds the same number later, a new link is created — history is preserved in the buyer's own converted purchase bills. Supplier's party list stays clean; buyer's records stay intact.

Multi-branch buyer with same phone number+

Scenario: A supplier saved the same buyer's mobile number under two different party names — two branches of the same business. A naive implementation merges them or shows one ledger with confusingly mixed transactions from both branches.

Resolution: Each party record surfaces as a completely independent ledger card. Supplier can toggle sharing per-card and track balances separately. On the buyer side, both cards appear in Shared Books with the supplier's name as the header — making it clear they're looking at two different credit lines from the same supplier.

Risk Area Failure modes we designed for
Payment timing

Buyer records a payment before it clears. Seller sees the entry and assumes money has arrived — and ships the next order against it.

A Pending Settlement status on payment entries with a visible timestamp. The seller knows the payment was logged and knows it hasn't settled — a distinction that matters with credit terms.

Off-platform cash

Cash changes hands. Nobody records it. The shared ledger shows an outstanding balance that both parties know isn't real.

Manual payment entry with proof attachment — a photo of the receipt, a UPI screenshot. Doesn't automate the problem away, but makes resolution fast enough that people will actually do it.

Partial adoption

Supplier is on ShareLedger. Buyer hasn't set it up. The supplier is sharing data into a void — single source of truth is still one-sided.

The design can't fully solve this — adoption is partly GTM. Our answer: contextual WhatsApp invite triggered when the supplier creates a voucher. Nudge arrives with a reason to act, not cold.

05 Discovery

Four entry points, one feature

Contextual banner on Parties screen

Shows once per device until dismissed. Copy: "Tired of asking for your party's ledger?" Targets suppliers who already have eligible pairs — the value proposition lands immediately because they already know the pain. Doesn't appear if user has already enabled the global "stop sharing" switch.

Persistent FAB with "NEW" badge

Floating Action Button remains visible on the Parties tab after the banner is dismissed. The NEW badge drops after the first interaction. For existing users returning post-release, this is often the first touchpoint they notice. Removed entirely if user enables the master privacy switch.

Full-screen onboarding modal on first post-release visit

Shown once per device to all users who land on the Parties screen after the feature ships. Lists the top three benefits in plain language. The CTA — "Explore Shared Ledgers" — deep-links directly to the FAB list. The modal can be dismissed and doesn't reappear.

In-app tutorial video in a bottom sheet

Accessible from a help icon on the shared ledger screen. Bottom sheet titled "How does shared ledger work?" embeds a short explainer video. Includes a direct CS call button and WhatsApp support chat shortcut — for users who want human help, not a FAQ. Added after CS flagged a pattern of users discovering the feature and then calling to ask what it meant.

06 Impact

What success looks like

The feature shipped Android first. The 30-day target is 10% of existing shared ledgers actively viewed at least once a week — conservative given the baseline of 591K already-paired ledgers that existed in the system.

Network effect via CPP

Every supplier who shares their ledger introduces their buyer to MyBillBook. That buyer sees the product working before they've signed up — with their own data already inside it. It's a quieter acquisition path than paid ads, but the intent is far higher.

Network effect · CPP strategy

None of the main competitors — Vyapar, Swipe, Zoho, Xero — offer a true two-way shared ledger yet.

The CPP (Counter-Party Platform) angle is the longer game: the buyer who discovers MyBillBook through a shared ledger is a warmer acquisition than any top-of-funnel marketing. They arrived because the product already had their data.
